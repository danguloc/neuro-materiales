Pyramidal & Extrapyramidal Motor Tracts
Vías Piramidal y Extrapiramidales

Preclinical Year 1–2 Unit: Descending Motor Pathways Reference: StatPearls / NBK554542 Dr. Daniel Angulo Conde
1

Overview & Key Concepts

Core idea: Voluntary movement depends on two major descending systems that originate in the cerebral cortex and converge on the final common pathway — the lower motor neuron (LMN) in the anterior horn of the spinal cord. Understanding where each tract runs, where it crosses, and what it controls allows you to localize lesions on neurological examination.

The pyramidal system (corticospinal tract) is the direct, fast-conducting pathway for skilled voluntary movement. Its name comes from the pyramid-shaped bulge it forms on the ventral medulla. The extrapyramidal system (EPS) is an anatomical concept first described by Johann Prus in 1898 and encompasses all descending motor pathways that do not pass through the medullary pyramids — primarily the reticulospinal, vestibulospinal, rubrospinal, and tectospinal tracts, as well as the basal ganglia circuitry that modulates them.

Despite their names, the two systems are functionally inseparable: pyramidal pathways execute voluntary commands while extrapyramidal circuits provide the background tone, postural adjustment, and automatic movement sequencing that make those commands fluent and purposeful.

Language tip: In English, "tract" refers to a bundle of axons with a common origin, destination, and function — equivalent to tracto in Spanish. "Pathway" (vía) is the broader functional concept; a single pathway may include multiple tracts.

The Pyramidal System — Corticospinal Tract

The corticospinal tract (tracto corticoespinal) originates predominantly from the primary motor cortex (area 4, precentral gyrus), with additional contributions from the premotor cortex (area 6), supplementary motor area, and somatosensory cortex. Upper motor neurons (UMN / neurona motora superior) project their axons through the posterior limb of the internal capsule, continue into the cerebral peduncles of the midbrain, travel through the pons, and finally form the medullary pyramids.

At the pyramidal decussation (decusación piramidal) — located at the junction of the medulla and spinal cord — approximately 85–90% of fibers cross the midline to form the lateral corticospinal tract in the contralateral dorsolateral funiculus. The remaining 10–15% descend uncrossed as the anterior corticospinal tract, which crosses segmentally via the anterior white commissure before synapsing.

Both divisions ultimately synapse on lower motor neurons (LMN / neurona motora inferior) or interneurons in Rexed laminae V, VI, VII, and IX of the anterior horn. The lateral tract predominantly controls distal limb musculature (fine hand and finger movements); the anterior tract controls axial and proximal limb musculature bilaterally.

The Extrapyramidal System — Four Key Tracts

The EPS is polysynaptic and composed of nuclei and fiber tracts that do not pass through the medullary pyramids. The four principal descending tracts are distinguished by origin, funiculus, and primary motor function.

1. Reticulospinal Tract (tracto reticuloespinal)
Medial (pontine) reticulospinal: originates in the pontine reticular formation → descends ipsilaterally in the anterior funiculus → activates extensor α and γ motor neurons. Also receives spinothalamic input.
Lateral (medullary) reticulospinal: originates in the medullary reticular formation → descends bilaterally in the lateral funiculus → modulates spinal motor neurons. Together these tracts integrate autonomic, respiratory, and voluntary motor commands.
2. Vestibulospinal Tract (tracto vestibuloespinal)
Medial vestibulospinal: from medial vestibular nucleus (Schwalbe nucleus) → terminates on upper-body and neck/forelimb motor neurons. Responsible for cervical reflex stabilization.
Lateral vestibulospinal: from lateral vestibular nucleus (Deiters' nucleus, pons) → descends ipsilaterally to Rexed laminae VII–VIII → activates extensor motor neurons while inhibiting flexors. Critical for antigravity posture and equilibrium reactions.
3. Rubrospinal Tract (tracto rubroespinal)
Originates from the red nucleus (nucleus ruber) of the midbrain tegmentum → crosses at the ventral tegmental decussation (caudal midbrain) → descends contralaterally in the dorsolateral lateral funiculus → synapses on α and γ motor neurons in laminae V, VI, VII.
Function: stimulates flexor muscles; cooperates with the corticospinal tract in fine control of hand and finger movements; maintains muscle tone. In humans this tract is less prominent than in other species and partially functionally replaced by the corticospinal system.
4. Tectospinal Tract (tracto tectoespinal)
Originates from the superior colliculus of the midbrain → receives retinal and cortical visual input → crosses ventromedial to the periaqueductal gray (PAG) → terminates in the contralateral anterior horn laminae VI, VII, VIII of cervical and upper thoracic spinal cord.
Function: coordinates rapid orienting movements of the head, neck, and eyes in response to sudden visual, auditory, or tactile stimuli. Clinically relevant in startle reflex and audiovisual orientation responses.

Clinical Case — Motor System Localization Step 1 of 5
Presentation

A 62-year-old right-handed man presents to the emergency department with sudden-onset weakness in his right arm and leg, along with mild slurred speech (dysarthria). The symptoms began approximately 2 hours ago while he was at rest. He has a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes.

Reasoning prompt: Based on the lateralization of deficits (right-sided weakness + dysarthria), where do you hypothesize the lesion is located — cortex, internal capsule, brainstem, or spinal cord?
Physical Examination

Neurological findings: right-arm drift on pronator drift test; grip strength 3/5 on the right vs. 5/5 on the left. Gait: right circumduction pattern. Deep tendon reflexes: 3+ (brisk) at right biceps, brachioradialis, patellar, and Achilles tendons. Babinski sign is positive on the right. No muscle fasciculations. Sensation is intact bilaterally.

Reasoning prompt: The combination of spasticity, hyperreflexia, and a positive Babinski indicates lesion of which neuron type — UMN or LMN? Which tract is most directly implicated?
Tract Analysis

The lateral corticospinal tract on the LEFT side is the most likely site of damage. Because the pyramidal decussation occurs at the caudal medulla, a lesion in the left cerebral hemisphere or left internal capsule produces contralateral (right-sided) UMN signs. The corticospinal tract carries fibers controlling both arm and leg, explaining the hemiplegia pattern.

The intact sensation rules out a Brown-Séquard hemicord lesion (which would combine ipsilateral motor loss with contralateral sensory loss). The presence of dysarthria (with preserved language comprehension) and bilateral cortical control of facial muscles suggests involvement near the corticobulbar fibers, consistent with an internal capsule or cortical lesion.

Reasoning prompt: If the lesion were below the pyramidal decussation (e.g., cervical cord), would the motor signs be ipsilateral or contralateral to the lesion side?
Extrapyramidal Contribution

Beyond the direct motor loss, the spasticity and exaggerated reflexes in this patient reflect disruption of descending inhibitory control from the reticulospinal and vestibulospinal tracts. In normal physiology, these EPS tracts modulate spinal interneuron excitability and inhibit excessive reflex activity. When the corticospinal system is acutely damaged, the imbalance between pontine reticulospinal facilitation (extensors) and medullary reticulospinal inhibition produces the characteristic spastic pattern.

In severe brainstem lesions (e.g., hemorrhage at the pontine level), the vestibulospinal and reticulospinal tracts become unopposed, producing decerebrate rigidity — extension of all limbs — because the rubrospinal inhibitory influence on extensors is also lost.

Reasoning prompt: What distinguishes decorticate posturing from decerebrate posturing in terms of which EPS tracts are preserved or lost?
Summary & Conclusion

This patient has a left-sided UMN (corticospinal) lesion, most likely an ischemic stroke in the territory of the left middle cerebral artery or involving the posterior limb of the left internal capsule. The clinical features — contralateral hemiplegia, spasticity, hyperreflexia, positive Babinski — are the classic signature of pyramidal system interruption above the pyramidal decussation.

Decorticate posturing (injury at the superior border of the red nucleus): upper extremity flexion + lower extremity extension. The rubrospinal and medullary reticulospinal tracts are overactivated; corticospinal and rubrospinal inhibition of extensors is partially preserved.

Decerebrate posturing (injury at the superior border of the pons): extension of all four limbs. All descending inhibitory cortical and rubrospinal input is lost; vestibulospinal and pontine reticulospinal tracts dominate.

✓ Key takeaway: Tract localization is always anatomically predictable. Crossing of the pyramidal tract at the caudal medulla means: cortical/internal capsule lesion → contralateral deficit; spinal cord lesion (below decussation) → ipsilateral deficit on the same side as the cord damage.
